# 2026-03-10 — What we actually have, and what would make it compelling

## What's genuinely novel

The fixed-lattice result alone is not novel. A competitive exclusion model on a toroid
falling into voter model universality class is expected and publishable only as a clean
characterization of a new model system. Interesting but not surprising.

The dynamic graph result is where novelty lives. The 7× ordering enhancement from
topology co-evolution is a clean, quantitative demonstration that homophily emerges
spontaneously from competitive dynamics without being designed in. That's not obvious.
Compatible nodes weren't told to seek each other — they rewired toward each other because
the rules made it locally advantageous. The result is a system that self-organizes both
its state *and* its connectivity simultaneously.

What makes this worth talking about is the combination: a baseline (fixed lattice, known
universality class) and a perturbation (topological freedom), and the perturbation
produces a dramatic, measurable shift. That's a controlled comparison. That's science.

## What it actually means

Three levels of ambition.

**Concrete level.** Adaptive networks — networks where topology and state co-evolve —
order dramatically more efficiently than fixed networks under the same dynamics. Direct
relevance to network science, opinion dynamics modeling, and theoretical neuroscience.
Real neural networks rewire. Real social networks rewire. Fixed-lattice models of those
systems are missing something important, and this system quantifies exactly how much.

**Middle level.** A toy model of how structure and function bootstrap each other. Nodes
don't just organize their states into domains — they organize their connectivity to
reinforce those domains. Once a domain forms, compatible connections accumulate around
it, making it more stable, harder for mutation to disrupt. The topology becomes a memory
of the state history. A genuinely interesting dynamical property.

**Ambitious level (requires care not to overclaim).** A concrete model of relational
self-organization with no background geometry. The graph that emerges isn't imposed —
it's a consequence of the rules. Whether that graph has properties relevant to emergent
geometry in quantum gravity is speculative but not empty speculation. The degree
distribution of the emergent graph, its clustering coefficient, its small-world
properties — measurable and comparable to theoretical predictions from causal set theory
and spin foam models.

## What would make it more compelling to share

Current state: phase diagrams and a τ measurement. Numbers. What makes a result land
with a broad audience is numbers plus a picture that makes the phenomenon immediately
visible.

**Missing piece 1: Direct visual comparison.**
Fixed lattice at mr=5 (critical point, scale-free domains) side-by-side with dynamic
graph at its equivalent critical point. If the dynamic graph shows visually tighter, more
sharply bounded domains — which the 7× ordering enhancement predicts — that image does
more communicative work than any table.

**Missing piece 2: Degree distribution at the dynamic graph critical point.**
If it's power-law, there's a scale-free network emerging at the same point where the
domain size distribution is scale-free. Two simultaneous power laws at the same critical
point is a striking result — it would suggest the topological and state transitions are
coupled, not independent. That would be the headline result.

**Missing piece 3: Universality class measurement for the dynamic graph.**
τ(L) analysis was completed for the fixed lattice (τ→2.0, voter model / 2D percolation).
Do the same for the dynamic graph. If τ is different — and the 7× ordering shift suggests
the universality class has likely changed — there's a clean before/after comparison
showing that topological freedom moves the system into a different universality class.
This is what would interest statistical physicists.

## The honest assessment

This is a real project with real results at the stage where most people stop — interesting
data, but the finding that makes it a story hasn't been extracted yet. The finding is:

*Competitive exclusion dynamics on a fixed lattice fall into voter model universality.
The same dynamics on an adaptive network produce dramatically stronger ordering and
likely a different universality class, driven by spontaneous homophily in the topology
co-evolution.*

That's a one-sentence result. Testable, quantitative, surprising, and it connects to
multiple active literatures. What stands between here and being able to say that
confidently is the universality class measurement on the dynamic graph side — the τ(L)
analysis just completed for the fixed lattice, repeated for the dynamic graph.

**That's the next step. Everything else is presentation.**
