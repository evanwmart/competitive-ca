## Finite-size scaling attempt

Ran the planned three-size sweep to extract critical exponents via
χ = N·Var(bd) scaling.

| Grid | Frames | Seeds | Jobs | Time |
|------|--------|-------|------|------|
| 64×64   | 50k | 8 | 168 | ~25 min |
| 256×256 | 15k | 8 | 168 | ~1.8 hr |

(128×128 / 50k / 8 seeds already in hand from sweep 4.)

Results:
- `results/sweep_64x64_f50000_s8_20260310_094014.{csv,png}`
- `results/sweep_256x256_f15000_s8_20260310_113908.{csv,png}`

### What we measured

Peak susceptibility χ = N · bd_var_mean at the variance peak for each (L, reinforce_min):

| L   | rm=2 χ | rm=3 χ | rm=4 χ |
|-----|--------|--------|--------|
| 64  | 0.668  | 0.717  | 1.117  |
| 128 | 0.701  | 0.727  | 0.936  |
| 256 | 0.911  | 0.979  | 1.039  |

### Null result

χ_peak is approximately constant across all three system sizes.  For a
standard continuous phase transition χ_peak ~ L^(γ/ν) with γ/ν > 0 — e.g.
×4 from L=64→256 for 2D Ising.  We see roughly flat or weakly increasing
values, with no consistent scaling exponent.

Separately, bd_mean is essentially identical across all three grid sizes for
the same (mr, reinforce_min).  Boundary density is an intensive quantity that
doesn't exhibit the system-size dependence FSS requires.

### 256×256 data unreliable

Seed-to-seed variance (bd_mean_std) is larger at L=256 than at L=64 or 128 —
the opposite of what self-averaging predicts for an equilibrated system.  The
256×256 runs used 15k frames (15k updates/node), against 50k/node for the
128×128 data.  A fair comparison requires 50k frames at 256×256, which takes
~8 hours.  We did not rerun.

### Interpretation

Three possibilities for the null χ scaling:

1. bd variance is the wrong observable — it saturates quickly with system size
   even near the critical point
2. The transition is a crossover at the sizes we can simulate, not a
   true thermodynamic phase transition
3. The correlation length ξ is already larger than our grid at all three sizes,
   placing all runs in the same finite-size regime

Pivoted to domain size distribution, which should be more sensitive.

## Domain size distribution — power-law analysis

### Infrastructure change

Added log2-binned histogram to stats CSV output.  The Stats struct already
computed `size_histogram[24]` (bin i covers domain sizes [2^i, 2^(i+1))); it
was just not being emitted.  Extended `print_stats_header` and `print_stats`
in `main.c` to append columns h0–h23.  Updated `sweep.py` to accept rows with
≥8 columns (was == 8).

Wrote `analysis/histogram.py`:
- Runs torus at each mutation rate, parses h0–h23 columns
- Averages histograms over frames (second half only) and over seeds
- Fits P(s) ~ s^(-τ) via log-log linear regression (scipy.stats.linregress)
  over bins 1–20 with nonzero counts
- Left panel: log-log P(s) vs s for each mutation rate with fit lines
- Right panel: τ and R² vs mutation probability on shared axis

### Run

128×128, 5000 frames, 4 seeds, reinforce_min=4, mr=3–9.

Results: `results/hist_128x128_f5000_s4_rm4_20260310_115500.png`

### Results

| mr | prob  | τ    | R²    |
|----|-------|------|-------|
| 3  | 0.333 | 2.43 | 0.939 |
| 4  | 0.250 | 2.29 | 0.952 |
| 5  | 0.200 | 1.89 | **0.961** |
| 6  | 0.167 | 1.62 | 0.922 |
| 7  | 0.143 | 1.54 | 0.890 |
| 8  | 0.125 | 1.48 | 0.901 |
| 9  | 0.111 | 1.41 | 0.835 |

### Findings

**R² peaks at mr=5 (prob=0.20).** The distribution is most power-law at this
mutation rate — a clean, unambiguous peak in fit quality.  This is the
sharpest critical-point locator we have found.  The earlier variance-peak
analysis placed the critical region at mr=4–7; this narrows it to mr≈5.

**τ ≈ 1.89 at the critical point.** τ varies monotonically: large (steep,
many small domains) in the disordered phase; small (flat, large domains
dominate) in the ordered phase.  At the peak R², τ sits near 1.89.

**Comparison to known universality classes:**

| Class | τ |
|---|---|
| 2D percolation | 187/91 ≈ 2.055 |
| 2D Ising (Fisher relation) | ≈ 2.05 |
| Our system at mr=5 | 1.89 ± ? |

τ ≈ 1.89 is below both standard 2D values.  This is either a genuinely
distinct universality class, a finite-size artefact suppressing the large-bin
tail (making the slope appear shallower), or a sign that the fit range needs
to be restricted to avoid finite-size cutoffs at large bins.  Distinguishing
these requires a larger grid.

**This observable behaves better than bd variance for critical-point
location.** The R² curve has a clear, narrow peak; the bd variance peak was
broad and did not scale cleanly with system size.

## Current state

The critical point is located at mr=5 (mutation probability ≈ 0.20) for
reinforce_min=4.  The transition is continuous.  The power-law exponent at the
critical point is τ ≈ 1.89, not obviously matching 2D percolation or Ising.

### Open questions

1. **Does τ sharpen on a larger grid?**  Run histogram analysis at 256×256
   with more frames to extend the power-law range to larger bin sizes.  If τ
   moves toward 2.05 it may be a finite-size effect; if it stays near 1.89
   the universality class is genuinely different.

2. **Does the critical point shift with reinforce_min?**  Run histogram.py
   with rm=2 and rm=3.  The sweep data showed rm=4 has the sharpest variance
   peak; the R²-vs-mr curve may sharpen or shift.

3. **Can FSS be recovered with a better observable?**  The peak domain count
   or peak bin count in the histogram may scale more cleanly with L than bd
   variance.  Worth attempting once a proper 256×256/50k run is in hand.

4. **What is the correlation length at mr=5?**  The crossover bin in the
   histogram — where P(s) departs from the power law — is a proxy for the
   largest coherent domain, hence for ξ.  Tracking this crossover bin vs mr
   would show whether ξ diverges as expected at the critical point.
