## Critical point located: mr=3 (prob ≈ 0.33)

Three simultaneous signals at mr=3:

**1. Variance spike.**
- mr=2: var = 1.0e-5
- mr=3: var = 8.4e-5  ← peak, 8× higher than mr=2, 21× higher than mr=4
- mr=4: var = 4.0e-6

This is the standard signal for a critical point.  Variance in bd peaks at the phase
transition because fluctuations are largest there.

**2. Boundary density collapse.**
bd drops from 0.581 (mr=2) to 0.104 (mr=3) — a factor of ~5.6 in one step.
The system goes from roughly disordered (bd≈0.58) to substantially ordered (bd≈0.10)
across a single integer step in mutation rate.

**3. Degree jump.**
Mean degree jumps from 3.59 (mr=2) to 7.30 (mr=3) — nearly doubling at the transition.

Note: degree in the disordered phase (mr=1,2) is *below* the initial degree of 4.
Severance dominates so strongly in the disordered phase that nodes lose connections
below their starting point.  The transition is not just ordering of state — it is a
simultaneous restructuring of the entire network topology.

## The first-order question

The degree jump from 3.59 → 7.30 across mr=2→3 is almost a factor of 2. For comparison,
bd changes by 5.6× but that is a continuous quantity with no obvious discontinuity test.
Degree is different: it is a structural quantity, and a discontinuous jump in mean degree
at the transition would indicate a **first-order (discontinuous) phase transition**.

Evidence for first-order:
- The degree jump is large and abrupt — no intermediate values between 3.6 and 7.3
- The seed-to-seed variance in bd at mr=3 is unusually wide (var range: 5.7e-5 to 1.15e-4)
  — bimodal behavior (some seeds on each side of the transition) is a first-order signature
- The bd drop of 5.6× is very abrupt compared to the gentle slopes on either side

Evidence against / for continuous:
- Variance peak at mr=3 is consistent with either first or second order
- Only one integer-valued mutation rate between the two phases; cannot rule out that the
  transition is sharp but continuous and we're just sampling coarsely

**This cannot be resolved from integer mutation rates alone.**
A sub-integer scan (mr=2.0, 2.2, 2.4, 2.6, 2.8, 3.0) would require fractional rates.
The current `mutation_rate` parameter is an integer. Options:
- Add fractional mutation rate to the binary (e.g. interpret as N/1000 fixed-point)
- Use larger integers and rescale: run at mr=20,22,24,...,30 with 10× larger grid?
- Accept the resolution limit and proceed to FSS (τ(L)) at mr=3

## Seed variation at mr=3 is itself a result

At mr=3, bd across 8 seeds: 0.1022, 0.1026, 0.1040, 0.1042, 0.1045, 0.1046, 0.1054, 0.1063.
The variance per seed varies 2× (5.7e-5 to 1.15e-4). This is much larger relative variation
than at any other mutation rate. Two interpretations:
1. The system is near a critical point with large finite-size fluctuations (consistent with
   second-order transition)
2. Some seeds land in the ordered basin, some closer to the disordered basin, reflecting
   metastability (consistent with first-order transition)

Distinguishing these requires either longer runs (do the two populations converge?) or
multiple system sizes (does variance scale as L^{γ/ν} or as L^d?).
