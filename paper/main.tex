\title{Adaptive rewiring produces sharp finite-size
       bimodality in a conviction-weighted
       competitive network}

\author{Evan W. Martin}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\begin{abstract}
We study a three-species competitive model in which each node
carries a continuous three-channel state and interactions favor
the locally dominant species.
The order parameter $\rb$ is the fraction of edges connecting
nodes of different dominant species, with low $\rb$ corresponding
to species-segregated order and high $\rb$ to a well-mixed
disordered state.
We ask how coupling these local dynamics to adaptive rewiring
changes the finite-size behavior of the mutation-driven
order--disorder transition.
At $\Lsys = 128$, the fixed toroidal lattice shows a smooth
crossover-like loss of order, while the adaptive network shows a
sharp jump from $\rb \approx 0.17$ to $\approx 0.48$ across
$\mu \in [0.35, 0.355]$ together with bimodal per-seed
distributions.
Finite-size Binder analysis disfavors conventional first-order
scaling over the tested sizes: the Binder dip weakens from
$\Lsys = 128$ to $\Lsys = 256$, the bimodal window narrows, and
the $\Lsys = 384$ result is consistent with this trend but
limited by equilibration.
The data are consistent with a sharp continuous transition over
the tested range, although a weakly first-order transition with
crossover length beyond $\Lsys = 384$ cannot be excluded.
Degree-cap controls indicate that degree headroom contributes
to the sharpening, but the mechanism remains partly confounded
by reinforcement-firing and formation-rate effects.
Adaptive topology thus emerges as a strong finite-size
sharpening mechanism in this conviction-weighted competitive
model.
The asymptotic order of the transition remains open.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Multi-species competition on lattices is a well-studied route to
spatial pattern formation.  Models of cyclic and non-cyclic
competition produce phase transitions between ordered
(species-segregated) and disordered (well-mixed) states whose
universality depends on lattice geometry, number of species,
and the form of the local
dynamics~\cite{szabo2007evolutionary,reichenbach2007mobility,szolnoki2014cyclic}.
On fixed regular lattices, these transitions are typically continuous.

Real populations, however, do not interact on static topologies.
Individuals form and break social, spatial, or ecological links
in response to their own state and the states of their
neighbors---a coevolutionary feedback between dynamics
\emph{on} the network and dynamics \emph{of} the
network~\cite{gross2008adaptive}.
A growing body of work shows that this feedback can qualitatively
change collective behavior, including the order of phase
transitions.
Holme and Newman~\cite{holme2006nonequilibrium} and
Vazquez et al.~\cite{vazquez2008generic} demonstrated that
adaptive rewiring converts the continuous fragmentation
transition in the voter model to a discontinuous one.
Gross et al.~\cite{gross2006epidemic} found analogous
sharpening in adaptive epidemic models, and
Demirel et al.~\cite{demirel2011cyclic} studied adaptive
networks with cyclic dominance, showing that coevolution
of topology and strategy leads to spontaneous
network fragmentation.

Here we ask whether topological coevolution changes the order
of the order--disorder transition in a conviction-weighted
multi-species competition model.
The transition we study is between a low-boundary-density,
species-segregated phase and a high-boundary-density, well-mixed
phase, driven by the mutation probability~$\mu$
(the boundary density $\rb$ is the fraction of edges connecting
nodes of different dominant species; defined precisely in
Section~\ref{sec:model}).
We further ask what specific feature of the rewiring drives any
change in transition character.
Our system differs from prior adaptive-network studies in three
respects:
(i)~node states are continuous three-channel vectors with a
    \emph{dominance margin} (the difference between the largest
    and second-largest channel) that determines who concedes in
    pairwise conflicts---a soft, ``conviction-weighted'' analogue
    of majority or voter dynamics; whether the continuous margin
    is essential to the phenomenology, as opposed to a
    discrete-state simplification, is left to future work;
(ii)~rewiring is globally non-local---like-state interactions
    trigger formation of an edge to a uniformly random non-neighbor
    anywhere in the system, subject to a degree cap, rather than
    being restricted to neighbors of neighbors;
(iii)~we compare directly against a fixed-lattice control to
    assess the effect of topological freedom.

We find that adaptive rewiring strongly sharpens the finite-size
transition relative to the fixed lattice: $\rb$ jumps and per-seed
distributions become bimodal at intermediate system sizes.
Binder-cumulant trends, however, weaken with $\Lsys$ rather than
strengthening, consistent with a sharp continuous transition over
the tested range and disfavoring a conventional first-order
interpretation; a weakly first-order alternative with crossover
length beyond $\Lsys = 384$ is not excluded.
Control experiments indicate that some degree headroom is
necessary for the sharp jump, though the effect is not cleanly
separable from reinforcement-firing and formation-rate confounds.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:model} defines the model.
Sections~\ref{sec:fixed} and~\ref{sec:adaptive} present results
on the fixed lattice and adaptive network, respectively,
including control experiments.
Section~\ref{sec:fss} presents finite-size behavior near the
sharp transition, including the Binder cumulant test.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the mechanism
and connections to related models.

\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}

The model is a random-sequential interacting particle system
with continuous internal states on an adaptive graph: nodes
carry continuous $[0,1]^3$ state vectors, updates are
\emph{asynchronous and random-sequential} (one randomly selected
node updates at a time, with each update seeing the current
configuration), and in the adaptive variant the topology
coevolves with the dynamics.
We refer to the dynamics as a ``cellular automaton'' loosely,
following the convention in the lattice competition literature;
in the strict sense the updates are not synchronous.

\subsection{Fixed lattice}

The substrate is an $\Lsys \times \Lsys$ two-dimensional torus:
a square grid with periodic boundary conditions in both directions,
giving every node exactly four neighbors.
This 4-regular toroidal lattice serves as the fixed-topology
control: periodic boundaries remove edge effects, so any
difference between fixed-lattice and adaptive-network behavior
is attributable to topological coevolution rather than to
boundary geometry.
The 4-regular degree also matches the initial mean degree of the
adaptive network at $t = 0$, so divergence in late-time behavior
reflects rewiring dynamics rather than initial-condition mismatch.

\subsection{Node state and competition}

Each node~$i$ carries a three-channel color value
$\bm{c}_i = (r_i, g_i, b_i) \in [0,1]^3$.
The three channels represent three competing species;
the \emph{dominant type} of node~$i$ is the channel
with the largest value.
Ties between channel values are uncommon: states are
initialized continuously and then updated in increments of
$\delta = 1/255$.
When ties occur they are resolved by a deterministic
implementation rule (documented in the public code).
Because ties are rare and the three species are otherwise
treated identically, the choice of tie-breaker does not affect
the reported phase behavior within numerical resolution; the
phase diagram is symmetric under species relabeling.
The continuous color values endow each node with a ``conviction'':
the \emph{dominance margin} is the difference between the
dominant channel and the maximum of the other two.
A node with color $(0.78, 0.12, 0.04)$ is strongly red
(margin~$0.67$), while $(0.35, 0.33, 0.31)$ is weakly red
(margin~$0.02$) and easily converted.

At each elementary step a node~$i$ is chosen uniformly at random
and interacts with each of its neighbors~$j$.
Channel values change in increments of
$\delta = 1/255 \approx 0.004$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Agreement} (same dominant type):
        both $i$ and~$j$ increment their shared dominant channel
        by~$\delta$ (clamped at~$1$).
  \item \textbf{Conflict} (different dominant types):
        the node with the smaller dominance margin shifts
        toward the winner's type:
        the winner's channel is incremented by~$\delta$ and the
        loser's own dominant channel is decremented by~$\delta$.
        When margins are equal, the focal node~$i$ concedes.
\end{itemize}

A collective reinforcement rule amplifies local consensus:
if at least $\rmin$ of~$i$'s neighbors share the same dominant
type (as determined by the edge relationship after competition),
that channel receives an additional increment.
All experiments use $\rmin = 4$ unless otherwise noted.

One \emph{frame} consists of $N = \Lsys^2$ elementary steps,
corresponding to one sweep of the lattice in expectation.

\subsection{Mutation}

After node~$i$ has interacted with all its neighbors and
reinforcement has been applied, $i$~is reset to a uniformly
random color---each channel drawn independently and uniformly
from $[0, 1]$---with probability~$\mu$.
Mutation is applied once per node activation, not once per
pairwise interaction.
This mutation is the noise parameter that drives the
phase transition: low~$\mu$ favors local order;
high~$\mu$ disrupts it.

\subsection{Adaptive topology}

The adaptive variant begins from the same 4-regular toroidal
lattice but allows the network to rewire.
After each pairwise interaction between nodes~$i$ and~$j$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item If the edge is \textbf{compatible} (endpoints share
        dominant type after competition) and the focal node $i$
        has degree $< \kmax$: with probability $1/N$, $i$ attempts
        to form a new edge to a non-neighbor.
        Up to~8 candidate indices are drawn uniformly at random
        from $\{0, \ldots, N{-}1\}$; a candidate is accepted if
        it is not~$i$ or~$j$, is not already a neighbor of~$i$,
        and itself has degree $< \kmax$.
        If none of the 8 candidates qualifies, the formation
        attempt is discarded.
        The new edge target is \emph{not} required to share~$i$'s
        dominant type: compatibility-triggered formation selects
        \emph{when} a node attempts to grow its neighborhood, and
        subsequent severance of incompatible edges then prunes
        the topology.
        Homophilic clustering therefore arises from
        compatibility-triggered formation plus selective survival,
        not from explicitly choosing same-type targets.
  \item If the edge is \textbf{incompatible} (endpoints differ):
        with probability $1/N$, the edge is severed, provided
        both endpoints retain degree $\geq 2$.
\end{itemize}
The maximum degree is capped at $\kmax = 8$ (twice the initial
lattice degree).
This cap is not a passive parameter: changing $\kmax$ alters
the equilibrium mean degree $\kavg$, which in turn changes the
number of pairwise interactions per node activation, the
reinforcement-firing probability under fixed $\rmin$, and the
per-frame rewiring opportunity (Section~\ref{sec:local_control}).
The minimum degree floor of~$2$ reduces the frequency of
isolated nodes and extreme fragmentation, but does not
guarantee global connectivity.

The rewiring probability is $1/N$ per pairwise interaction, and
each frame contains $O(N\kavg)$ pairwise interactions
(${\sim}N$ activations $\times$ $O(\kavg)$ neighbors each), so
the expected number of rewiring events per frame is $O(\kavg)$,
\emph{independent of $N$ for bounded degree}.
Extensive topological relaxation therefore requires $O(N)$
frames.

Each run is initialized from a 64-bit seed; the seed deterministically
sets the initial colors and the state of the pseudo-random number
generator used for all subsequent uniform draws (node selection,
mutation, candidate sampling, severance acceptance).
Two runs with the same seed and parameters produce identical
trajectories.

In the ordered phase ($\mu < \mc$), nodes cluster near
the degree cap: at $\mu = 0.33$, approximately $82\%$ of
nodes have $k = 8$ and $95\%$ have $k \geq 7$.
In the disordered phase ($\mu > \mc$), the distribution
shifts toward the floor: at $\mu = 0.50$, approximately
$44\%$ of nodes have $k = 2$ and $71\%$ have $k \leq 3$.
The degree distribution is thus strongly phase-dependent
(Fig.~\ref{fig:dyn_phase}, Table~\ref{tab:adaptive}).

The result is that ordered regions recruit long-range connections
while disordered boundaries shed them.
The topology and dynamics coevolve: the network is
simultaneously the arena for competition and a product of it.
A control variant restricts new-edge formation to nodes within
graph distance~2 of the focal node while keeping all other
rules identical (Section~\ref{sec:local_control}).

\subsection{Observables}

The primary order parameter is the \emph{boundary density}
$\rb$: the fraction of edges connecting nodes of different
dominant types.
Low $\rb$ ($\lesssim 0.15$) indicates species-segregated order;
high $\rb$ ($\gtrsim 0.5$) indicates a well-mixed disordered state.

On the adaptive network, the mean degree $\kavg$ serves as a
second, coupled order parameter: $\kavg \approx 7$--$8$ in the
ordered phase (near the cap) and $\kavg \approx 3$--$4$ in the
disordered phase (near the floor).

All reported values are time-averages over the second half of
each run (discarding transients).
Error bars on mean quantities in figures show the standard
error of the mean ($\sigma / \sqrt{n}$) across independent
seeds.
Tables report the seed-to-seed standard deviation~$\sigma$
and population variance.
The primary experiments use 64 seeds; the Binder cumulant
analysis uses 64 seeds per $\mu$ point at every system
size, including $\Lsys = 384$.

\subsection{Note on the reinforcement threshold}

On the fixed 4-regular lattice, $\rmin = 4$ requires all four
neighbors to agree before reinforcement fires---effectively
a unanimity rule.
On the adaptive network, where degree can reach $\kmax = 8$,
the same $\rmin = 4$ requires only half the neighbors to agree.
This asymmetry is inherent to applying a fixed threshold
across variable-degree topologies.
We address its consequences with a control experiment
(Section~\ref{sec:rmin_control}) showing that varying~$\rmin$
on the fixed lattice does not change the transition character.

\section{Fixed lattice: smooth crossover at $\Lsys = 128$}
\label{sec:fixed}

On the fixed 4-regular torus, at the tested system size
($\Lsys = 128$, 64 seeds, 50\,000 frames), boundary density
decreases smoothly with decreasing mutation probability
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fixed_phase}, Table~\ref{tab:fixed}).
No discontinuity is observed at any $\mu$ tested:
$\rb$ ranges from $0.48$ at $\mu = 0.33$
to $0.08$ at $\mu = 0.10$, with a smooth crossover
near $\mu \approx 0.20$.
This section serves as a baseline: the tested fixed-lattice
behavior at $\Lsys = 128$ is smooth, and
Section~\ref{sec:adaptive} asks what changes under adaptive
rewiring.
We do not claim this as a thermodynamic statement about the
fixed-lattice transition; multi-size fixed-lattice scaling
is left to future work.

\section{Adaptive network: sharp finite-size transition}
\label{sec:adaptive}

Allowing edges to coevolve qualitatively changes the
finite-size transition relative to the fixed-lattice baseline.

\subsection{Sharp finite-size jump in coupled order parameters}

The phase diagram of the adaptive network
(Fig.~\ref{fig:dyn_phase}, Table~\ref{tab:adaptive}) shows
both order parameters changing sharply between
$\mu = 0.35$ and $\mu = 0.355$.
At $\mu = 0.35$, the majority of seeds converge to the
ordered phase ($\rb \approx 0.17$, $\kavg \approx 6.0$);
at $\mu = 0.355$, the per-seed distribution is bimodal
(see Section~\ref{sec:topo_indep}), and the reported seed
mean $\rb \approx 0.48$, $\kavg \approx 4.2$ is therefore
not a typical single-seed value but the mean over a
two-basin distribution.
By $\mu = 0.36$, all seeds are fully disordered
($\rb = 0.55$, $\kavg = 3.9$).
The boundary density increases by a factor of ${\sim}3\times$
and the mean degree drops by ${\sim}1.5\times$ across this
narrow window.

\subsection{Topology-independent confirmation and finite-size coexistence}
\label{sec:topo_indep}

Because $\rb$ depends on the edge set (both numerator and
denominator), a sharp jump in $\rb$ alone could in principle
be a topological artifact of changing connectivity rather than
of the node states.
We rule this out with a state-only observable: the dominant-species
fraction $f_\mathrm{dom} = \max(n_R, n_G, n_B)/N$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fdom}), which drops sharply from $\approx 0.9$
to $\approx 1/3$ across the same window---identical jump
location, no edge set in the definition---confirming the
transition reflects a genuine state change.

Histograms of per-seed $\rb$ across the transition
(Fig.~\ref{fig:hist_coex}) reveal the distribution shape
more precisely than the scatter plots above.
At $\mu = 0.33$, the distribution is a tight unimodal
peak near $\rb \approx 0.10$.
At $\mu = 0.345$, it remains unimodal but broadens.
At $\mu = 0.35$, the distribution develops a long right tail,
with most seeds near $\rb \approx 0.16$ but a few
reaching $\rb > 0.3$.
At $\mu = 0.355$, the distribution is \emph{clearly bimodal}:
one cluster near $\rb \approx 0.15$ and a second
near $\rb \approx 0.55$, separated by a gap with no seeds.
By $\mu = 0.37$, all seeds have collapsed to a tight
unimodal peak in the disordered phase.

The bimodality at $\mu = 0.355$---with two well-separated
peaks and an empty gap---is the strongest coexistence-like
signature at $\Lsys = 128$.
Section~\ref{sec:binder} shows that these signatures
weaken with increasing system size.

\subsection{Variance spike and initial-condition dependence}
\label{sec:variance_spike}

The \emph{seed-to-seed variance of the per-run second-half
time-averaged $\rb$} peaks sharply at $\mu = 0.355$, with
$\mathrm{var}(\rb) = 1.5 \times 10^{-2}$, against an
off-transition background of $\mathrm{var}(\rb) \sim 10^{-7}$
in both phases (Table~\ref{tab:adaptive};
Fig.~\ref{fig:dyn_phase}, inset).
The off-transition background reflects tight single-basin
sampling within seeds; the peak reflects seeds splitting between
two basins.
We use the spike to \emph{localize} the transition, not as a
diagnostic of its order: a peak in seed-to-seed variance occurs
at both continuous and first-order transitions, so the magnitude
of the ratio to the off-transition value reflects the basin
separation rather than the transition order.

To test for initial-condition dependence, we run at
$\mu = 0.34$, $0.35$, and $0.36$ from both random and
spatially ordered initial conditions
(64 seeds each; Table~\ref{tab:hysteresis}).
At $\mu = 0.34$, both conditions converge to order;
at $\mu = 0.36$, both converge to disorder.
At $\mu = 0.35$, a small fraction of seeds escape to the
opposite basin (3/64 random-init, 2/64 ordered-init),
with means close in both cases
($\bar{\rb} = 0.175$ vs.\ $0.178$).
This is weak initial-condition dependence rather than
strong hysteresis.

\subsection{Controls}
\label{sec:local_control}

To isolate which features of the rewiring drive the
sharpening, we ran two families of controls: a local-only
control restricting new edges to distance-2 neighbors
(all other rules identical), and a degree-cap sweep
varying $\kmax$.

Figure~\ref{fig:threeway} compares four systems at
$\Lsys = 128$ (64 seeds, 30\,000 frames): the fixed lattice,
the local-only adaptive network ($\kmax = 8$), the global
adaptive network with $\kmax = 4$ (matching the fixed-lattice
degree), and the standard global adaptive network with
$\kmax = 8$.

On the local-only network, $\rb$ rises smoothly from
$\approx 0.13$ at $\mu = 0.33$ to $\approx 0.48$ at
$\mu = 0.50$, with no sharp jump at any $\mu$ tested.
The restriction removes the sharp jump, but it also shrinks
the candidate non-neighbor pool from $O(N) \approx 16{,}000$
system-wide to $O(k^2) \approx 50$--$60$ within graph
distance~2 at $\kmax = 8$, so it does not isolate non-locality
from formation rate.

A second family of controls varies the degree cap.
At $\kmax = 4$ on the global adaptive network (matching the
fixed-lattice degree), the sharp finite-size jump vanishes: $\rb$
rises smoothly from $\approx 0.15$ ($\mu = 0.15$) to
$\approx 0.56$ ($\mu = 0.50$), with seed-to-seed variance
three orders of magnitude below the $\kmax = 8$ case.
Because the network starts at degree~$4 = \kmax$, there is
no headroom for edge accumulation: at initialization every
node is already at the cap, so formation attempts fail until
severance opens a slot, and mean degree drops monotonically
to $\kavg \approx 3.0$--$3.7$.

To disentangle headroom from outright formation suppression,
we ran intermediate-headroom controls at $\kmax = 5$ and
$\kmax = 6$ (one and two units of headroom respectively).
Both recover a sharp coexistence-like jump
(Fig.~\ref{fig:threeway}):
at $\kmax = 5$, $\rb$ jumps from $\approx 0.20$ at
$\mu = 0.27$ to $\approx 0.51$ at $\mu = 0.30$;
at $\kmax = 6$, the jump shifts to between
$\mu = 0.30$ and $\mu = 0.33$
(from $\rb \approx 0.14$ to $\approx 0.53$);
at $\kmax = 8$, the jump occurs near $\mu \approx 0.345$.
The transition location drifts monotonically toward higher
$\mu$ with increasing headroom.
Over the tested values, the sharp jump is absent at
$\kmax = 4$ and present for $\kmax \geq 5$, suggesting a
threshold-like onset of the sharp coexistence-like
behavior once some degree headroom is available.

Together with the fixed-lattice baseline and the local-only
control, this $\kmax$ sweep is \emph{consistent with} a
headroom requirement: some room for degree to accumulate in
ordered regions appears necessary for the sharp jump, though
the
full $\kmax = 8$ headroom of the standard setting is not.
The reading is not, however, an unconfounded measurement of
headroom alone.
Three coupled effects vary together with $\kmax$:
(i)~the equilibrium ordered-phase mean degree changes
($\kavg \approx 3.1, 4.8, 5.8, 7.9$ at
$\kmax = 4, 5, 6, 8$), so the per-activation interaction
count varies;
(ii)~the reinforcement-firing probability under fixed
$\rmin = 4$ varies with $\kavg$ (it is structurally suppressed
at $\kmax = 4$ where $\kavg < \rmin$, and the effective ratio
$\rmin/\kavg$ varies from ${\approx}0.83$ to $0.51$ across
$\kmax \in \{5, 6, 8\}$); and
(iii)~the per-frame rewiring opportunity scales with $\kavg$.
A monotonic shift of the jump location with $\kmax$ is
therefore consistent with several mechanisms beyond pure
topological headroom.
The local-only control is similarly confounded by
candidate-pool shrinkage from $O(N)$ to $O(k^2)$.
Cleanly isolating any one of these factors would require an
$\rmin$ sweep on the adaptive network at fixed $\kmax = 8$
and a matched-formation-rate non-local control, both left to
future work.

\subsection{Reinforcement-threshold control}
\label{sec:rmin_control}

To probe an effective-threshold artifact ($\rmin = 4$ is
unanimity at degree~4 but only a $50\%$ majority at degree~8),
we repeated the fixed-lattice experiment with $\rmin = 2$
(64 seeds, 50\,000 frames), matching the fractional threshold
of $\rmin = 4$ at degree~8.
The $\Lsys = 128$ fixed-lattice curve remains smooth under this
match, and $\rb$ values shift downward only slightly.
The smooth fixed-lattice behavior therefore survives a
fractional-threshold match to the adaptive network, so the
fixed-lattice/adaptive contrast is not produced by the
threshold mismatch alone.
A separate confound---the dynamic variation of $\rmin/\kavg$
with phase on the adaptive network---is not addressed here
and is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}.

\section{Finite-size behavior near the sharp transition}
\label{sec:fss}

This section presents two complementary finite-size scans:
a bistability scan at fixed $\mu = 0.35$ across
$\Lsys \in \{64, 128, 256\}$ to show that seeds split between
basins at accessible sizes (this subsection), and a denser
Binder-cumulant scan across a finer $\mu$ grid with twice the
seed count to test how this finite-size signature evolves with
system size (Section~\ref{sec:binder}).

\paragraph{Bistability at fixed $\mu$}
We measure $\rb$ at $\mu = 0.35$ for $\Lsys = 64$, $128$,
and~$256$, with both random and ordered initial conditions
(32~seeds each; Table~\ref{tab:fss}).

At $\Lsys = 64$, all 64 seeds converge tightly to the ordered
phase ($\rb \approx 0.10$, $\kavg \approx 7.8$,
combined $\mathrm{var}(\rb) = 7.1 \times 10^{-7}$).
At $\Lsys = 128$, seeds split between basins with
$\mathrm{var}(\rb) = 3.8 \times 10^{-3}$.
At $\Lsys = 256$ (100\,000 frames for equilibration),
most seeds have crossed to the disordered side
($\bar{\rb} \approx 0.46$) but variance remains high
($\mathrm{var}(\rb) = 1.4 \times 10^{-2}$),
indicating that the system is still bistable at this size.

The combined variance \emph{increases} by four orders of
magnitude from $\Lsys = 64$ to $\Lsys = 256$.
At $\mu = 0.35$ the pseudocritical region drifts toward
this value with increasing~$\Lsys$
(Section~\ref{sec:binder}), so part of the variance
increase reflects the approach to the pseudocritical point.
Nonetheless, the strong broadening and bimodal splitting
at $\Lsys = 128$ and $256$ indicate coexistence-like
behavior at accessible system sizes.

\subsection{Binder cumulant}
\label{sec:binder}

The Binder analysis is a separate, denser scan than the
bistability check above: a fine $\mu$ grid
($\Delta\mu = 0.001$) with 64 seeds per $(\Lsys, \mu)$
point---twice the per-condition seed count of the
bistability scan---at $\Lsys \in \{64, 128, 256, 384\}$.
We compute the Binder cumulant
$U_\Lsys = 1 - \langle \rb^4 \rangle /
(3 \langle \rb^2 \rangle^2)$
from the second-half time-averaged $\rb$ value of each
independent seed (one value per seed), with the moments
$\langle \cdot \rangle$ taken across the seed ensemble.
For a tight single-peaked distribution $U_\Lsys \to 2/3$;
when the distribution is bimodal, $U_\Lsys$ develops a dip
below $2/3$.
For a conventional first-order transition the bimodal peaks
survive $\Lsys \to \infty$, so $U_\mathrm{min}$ should remain
below $2/3$ (the bounded support $\rb \in [0, 1]$ means it
need not diverge as in the unbounded-magnetization case, but
it should not recover toward the single-peak value).
An upward trend toward $2/3$ over the size range where the
trend is measured is therefore inconsistent with first-order
behavior.

\paragraph{Caveats specific to a one-sided order parameter}
Standard Binder analysis is formulated for a symmetric order
parameter (e.g., Ising magnetization, where the disordered
phase has $\langle m \rangle = 0$ and the ordered phase has
$|m| \neq 0$, so $U_\Lsys$ takes different limits in the two
phases and its curves cross near $\mc$).
Here $\rb \in [0, 1]$ is one-sided: it has nonzero mean in
both phases, so a tight ordered single peak ($\rb \approx 0.1$)
and a tight disordered single peak ($\rb \approx 0.55$) both
give $U_\Lsys \to 2/3$.
Only bimodality drives $U_\Lsys$ below $2/3$.
Consequently the diagnostic content of $U_\Lsys$ in this paper
is the dip \emph{depth} as a function of $\Lsys$, not a
universal-value crossing of $U_\Lsys$ curves at $\mc$, which
the standard analysis is not set up to deliver here.

Figure~\ref{fig:binder} shows $U_\Lsys$ vs.\ $\mu$ for
$\Lsys = 64$, $128$, $256$, and $384$.
Run lengths are 30\,000--50\,000 frames at
$\Lsys \in \{64, 128\}$, $100\,000$ frames at $\Lsys = 256$,
and $200\,000$ frames at $\Lsys = 384$.
For $\Lsys \in \{128, 256\}$ this roughly tracks $\Lsys^2$
scaling, and qualitative time-series spot-checks
(Supplementary Material, Sec.~S3) suggest comparable coverage
of $\tau_\mathrm{eq}$ at the two sizes.\footnote{%
A quantitative integrated-autocorrelation-time measurement
of $\rb$ vs.\ $\Lsys$ would put this on a firmer footing and
is part of the future-work list (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).}

At $\Lsys = 384$ run lengths fall short of the same scaling:
matching the per-$\tau_\mathrm{eq}$ coverage of $\Lsys = 256$
under a plausible dynamical exponent $z \gtrsim 2$ would
require ${\sim}225\,000$ frames or more, so the $200\,000$-frame
$\Lsys = 384$ runs cover fewer autocorrelation times than the
smaller-size runs, and time-series spot-checks confirm
within-run basin-switching similar to $\Lsys = 256$
(Supplementary Material, Sec.~S3).
The $\Lsys = 384$ point should therefore be treated as
supporting but not as load-bearing: incomplete sampling of
basin residence times can bias the per-seed Binder estimate,
and the direction of that bias depends on the balance between
trapped single-basin trajectories (which push $U_\mathrm{min}$
toward $2/3$) and trajectories that switch during the averaging
window (which can push it the other way).
The trend that drives the conclusions below is therefore the
$\Lsys = 128 \to 256$ comparison, where run lengths more
nearly track the expected $\Lsys^2$-like scaling.

The \emph{position} of the minimum shifts to lower~$\mu$ with
increasing~$\Lsys$:
$\mu^*(64) \approx 0.360$,
$\mu^*(128) \approx 0.352$,
$\mu^*(256) \approx 0.349$,
$\mu^*(384) \approx 0.348$.
The drift slows with increasing~$\Lsys$, consistent with
convergence toward a finite limiting value, though four points
do not permit a reliable extrapolation.
The measured dip values are (bootstrap 95\% CI in brackets,
64 seeds at each size):
$U_\mathrm{min} = -0.39\;[-0.85, -0.05]$ ($\Lsys = 64$),
$0.26\;[0.18, 0.36]$ ($\Lsys = 128$),
$0.52\;[0.49, 0.55]$ ($\Lsys = 256$),
and $0.58\;[0.56, 0.61]$ ($\Lsys = 384$).
Confidence intervals are computed by resampling per-seed $\rb$
values with replacement (seed-level non-parametric bootstrap,
2000 resamples; $U_\mathrm{min}$ recomputed on each resample
and the 2.5/97.5 percentiles reported).
The load-bearing finding is the $\Lsys = 128 \to 256$ change:
$U_\mathrm{min}$ moves from $0.26$ to $0.52$ (CIs
non-overlapping), a substantial fraction of the way to the
single-peak value $2/3$ at run lengths that roughly satisfy
$\Lsys^2$ scaling.
This is inconsistent with a first-order Binder dip, which
should not approach the single-peak value at larger~$\Lsys$.
The $\Lsys = 64$ point lies in the same direction but the
system contains only 4096 nodes; small-system corrections
plausibly dominate, and it is consistent with the
$\Lsys = 128 \to 256$ trend only if those corrections are
accepted.
The $\Lsys = 384$ point is also consistent with the trend but,
as discussed above, is downgraded given the equilibration
limitation.
Taken together, the data disfavor a conventional first-order
interpretation over the tested sizes and are consistent with
a sharp continuous transition.
We do not claim to have established a continuous transition:
a weakly first-order transition with a Binder crossover length
beyond $\Lsys = 384$ would mimic the same finite-size signature,
and settling the asymptotic order would require systems
substantially beyond $\Lsys = 384$ and longer run lengths.

Per-seed $\rb$ distributions at $\Lsys = 384$
(Supplementary Material, Sec.~S2) show that bimodality
persists at the largest size but is confined to a narrow
window $\mu \in [0.347, 0.349]$ around the Binder
minimum---substantially narrower than the $\Lsys = 128$
bimodal window of Figure~\ref{fig:hist_coex}, consistent
with the same sharpening-yet-continuous trend as the
Binder recovery toward $2/3$.

As a consistency check we attempted a two-parameter Binder
collapse $U_\Lsys(\mu) = \tilde{U}((\mu - \mc)\Lsys^{1/\nu})$.
The collapse fails for $\Lsys \in \{128, 256, 384\}$ (the
$\Lsys = 128$ dip is too deep to be absorbed by any
rescaling, consistent with $\Lsys = 128$ lying outside the
leading-order finite-size scaling regime).
Restricting to two curves $\Lsys \in \{256, 384\}$ yields a
tight collapse, but two curves with two free parameters
provide essentially no constraint on the exponent (bootstrap
95\% CI $1/\nu \in [0.97, 1.99]$ spans the standard
two-dimensional continuous-transition exponents without
selecting one).
We therefore do not assign a universality class from this
analysis and treat the collapse only as a consistency check;
see Supplementary Material, Sec.~S1 for the full procedure
and figure.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Mechanistic interpretation}
\label{sec:mechanism}

The control experiments in Section~\ref{sec:local_control}
narrow the set of topological ingredients that contribute
to the sharp finite-size transition at $\Lsys = 128$.
Sweeping $\kmax \in \{4, 5, 6, 8\}$ shows that any degree
headroom (one unit or more) recovers the sharp
coexistence-like jump, with the jump location shifting
monotonically toward higher~$\mu$ as headroom grows.
The local-only control also restores smoothness but is
confounded by candidate-pool shrinkage from $O(N)$ to
$O(k^2)$ and reduced effective formation rate;
non-local formation per se is implicated only indirectly.
Whether the formation/severance asymmetry contributes
beyond non-local formation alone has not been tested
directly (the converse control, making severance also
non-local, remains future work).

A second confound bounds the headroom interpretation.
At $\kmax = 4$, mean degree in the ordered phase drops to
$\kavg \approx 3.1$, below the reinforcement threshold
$\rmin = 4$; reinforcement firing is therefore
physically suppressed by construction at $\kmax = 4$,
not by absence of a homophily--topology feedback loop
alone.
The $\kmax = 4$ vs.\ $\kmax \geq 5$ contrast partly
reflects this trivial reinforcement-firing-rate effect.
A more interpretable signal is the monotonic $\mu$-shift
of the jump location across $\kmax \in \{5, 6, 8\}$,
where $\kavg \approx 4.8, 5.8, 7.9$ in the ordered phase
(all above $\rmin$) and the jump location moves from
$\mu \approx 0.30$ to $0.345$ with increasing headroom.
The direction of this shift is consistent with the
homophily--topology picture: more headroom yields larger
ordered-phase $\kavg$, so each node experiences more
reinforcement firings per frame in the ordered phase,
making it more robust against mutation and shifting
$\mc$ upward.
Even within the $\kmax \geq 5$ regime the effective ratio
$\rmin/k$ varies from $\approx 0.83$ to $0.51$, so the
shift cannot be cleanly attributed to headroom alone;
a definitive disentanglement would require an
$\rmin$ sweep on the adaptive network at fixed
$\kmax = 8$, which we leave to future work.

\paragraph{Interpretation: a homophily--topology feedback loop}
We offer the following picture as an interpretation of the data
above, not as an independently established mechanism.
In the ordered phase, compatible neighbors trigger formation
events that accumulate degree ($\kavg \approx 7$--$8$ at
$\kmax = 8$), and the larger neighborhood provides both more
reinforcement firings and more competition partners of the
same type, stabilizing order against mutation;
in the disordered phase, incompatibility drives severance,
$\kavg$ falls toward the floor ($\approx 3$--$4$), and sparse
connectivity makes spontaneous ordering difficult.
At intermediate system sizes this positive feedback produces
the coexistence-like signatures of
Section~\ref{sec:adaptive}; the Binder cumulant trend
(Section~\ref{sec:binder}) shows that these signatures weaken
at larger~$\Lsys$, indicating that any effective free-energy
barrier between basins does not grow unboundedly with system
size.
This picture is qualitatively consistent with prior
adaptive-network results in voter, SIS, and cyclic-dominance
models~\cite{holme2006nonequilibrium,vazquez2008generic,gross2006epidemic,min2017fragmentation,marceau2010adaptive,demirel2011cyclic,durrett2012graph},
where positive feedback between state coherence and network
structure has been identified as a generic sharpening
mechanism.
The loop predicts a directional, falsifiable signal: an
$\rmin$ sweep at fixed $\kmax = 8$ (which decouples the
reinforcement threshold from $\kavg$ without changing the
topology dynamics) should shift~$\mc$ \emph{downward} as
$\rmin$ rises---because fewer firings per frame in the
ordered phase reduces the mutation rate the system can
absorb before losing order---and \emph{upward} as $\rmin$
falls.
Testing this is left to future work.

\subsection{Limitations and future work}

Several aspects of this study would benefit from further
investigation.

\paragraph{Equilibration near $\mc$}
Time-series spot-checks at $\Lsys = 256$ and $\Lsys = 384$
show that some seeds undergo within-run basin-switching
during the averaging window, so second-half averages near
the Binder minimum partly conflate the two basins
(Supplementary Material, Sec.~S3).
The $\Lsys = 384$ run length is short of the $\Lsys^z$
scaling that would match $\Lsys = 256$ coverage under a
plausible $z \gtrsim 2$; this is the reason the
$\Lsys = 384$ Binder value is treated as supporting rather
than load-bearing (Section~\ref{sec:binder}).
A quantitative integrated-autocorrelation-time measurement
of $\rb$ vs.\ $\Lsys$ and longer $\Lsys = 384$ runs would
clarify both effects.

\paragraph{Statistical uncertainty in fourth-moment
quantities}
The 64-seed Binder analysis is on the low end for a
cumulant dominated by tail behavior of the per-seed $\rb$
distribution.
The bootstrap CIs reported in Section~\ref{sec:binder}
estimate sampling uncertainty conditional on the run length
used; they do not capture equilibration uncertainty, because
resampling partially-equilibrated samples preserves the
equilibration bias.
A higher seed count (e.g.\ $\geq 128$) at $\Lsys = 384$
together with the longer runs above would tighten both
sources of uncertainty.

\paragraph{Universality class}
The two-parameter Binder collapse at
$\Lsys \in \{256, 384\}$ is consistent with standard
two-dimensional continuous-transition exponents but is
weakly constrained by only two curves
(Supplementary Material, Sec.~S1).
A definitive universality-class assignment would require
simulations at $\Lsys \geq 512$ or correction-to-scaling
fits with additional free parameters.

\paragraph{Mechanism isolation}
The mechanism claim rests on ablation controls, not on a
direct measurement of the feedback loop.
The single highest-value missing control is an $\rmin$
sweep on the adaptive network at fixed $\kmax = 8$
(Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}), which would disentangle
degree headroom from the reinforcement-firing-rate effect.
A matched-formation-rate non-local control and a
non-local-severance control would further sharpen the
roles of formation range and the formation/severance
asymmetry, respectively.

\paragraph{Discrete-state ablation}
The model uses a continuous dominance margin
(``conviction-weighted'' rule) for resolving conflicts.
Whether this continuous margin is essential to the
phenomenology, or whether a discrete three-state variant
(e.g.\ majority-update or voter-style conflict resolution)
on the same adaptive graph reproduces the sharp finite-size
behavior, has not been tested.
A near-term followup is to run such a discrete-state variant
under otherwise identical rules and parameters and compare
the Binder trends.

\paragraph{Mean-field theory}
A mean-field analysis for the coupled $\rb$--$\kavg$
dynamics would help anchor the simulation results and
predict the location of the bistable window as a function
of model parameters.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\paragraph{What was found}
At $\Lsys = 128$ the fixed toroidal lattice shows a smooth
crossover-like loss of order.
Adaptive non-local rewiring sharpens the same local rules
into a coexistence-like finite-size jump in coupled order
parameters ($\rb: 0.17 \to 0.48$, $\kavg: 6.0 \to 4.2$ across
$\mu \in [0.35, 0.355]$), bimodal per-seed distributions at
intermediate system sizes, and a sharply localized
seed-to-seed variance peak.
Between $\Lsys = 128$ and $\Lsys = 256$ the Binder minimum
moves from $0.26$ to $0.52$ (CIs non-overlapping, run lengths
roughly satisfying $\Lsys^2$ scaling)---a substantial fraction
of the way to the single-peak value $2/3$.
This is the load-bearing evidence: it disfavors a conventional
first-order interpretation over the tested sizes and is
consistent with a sharp continuous transition.
A degree-cap sweep is consistent with a degree-headroom
requirement, with the jump location drifting monotonically
toward higher~$\mu$ as headroom grows.
The mechanism reading is consistent with the data but does not
rest on an unconfounded measurement; the limits are listed next.

\paragraph{What remains open}
We do not claim to have established a continuous transition.
A weakly first-order alternative with crossover length beyond
$\Lsys = 384$ would mimic the same finite-size signature, and
the $\Lsys = 384$ Binder estimate is limited by fewer sampled
autocorrelation times than the smaller-size runs.
The mechanism is also not fully isolated: changing $\kmax$
changes degree headroom, reinforcement firing, interaction
count, and rewiring opportunity simultaneously, while the
local-only control also changes the candidate pool.
Adaptive topology therefore emerges as a strong finite-size
sharpening mechanism in this conviction-weighted competitive
model, while the asymptotic order of the transition and the
clean separation of rewiring ingredients remain open.
